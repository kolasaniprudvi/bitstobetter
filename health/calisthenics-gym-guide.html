Pull-Ups: The King of Bodyweight Exercises

Setup & Starting Position

  1. Stand below a pull-up bar with shoulder-width grip
  2. Hang with arms fully extended; scapula relaxed (not shrugged)
  3. Engage your core; slight PPT (posterior pelvic tilt)
  4. Feet should be under bar or slightly behind (avoid forward swing)

Main Movement (Concentric)

  1. Initiate with scapular retraction: Pull shoulder blades down & back
  2. Flex elbows: Keep elbows close to torso (not flared 45° out)
  3. Lead with chest: Lean back ~15° to engage lats
  4. Target end position: Chest near bar, chin above bar

🔬 Biomechanics: Scapular Engagement

The pull-up motion has two distinct phases:

Phase 1: Scapular Pull (0-3 inches)

Your lats and rhomboids retract the scapula. This is the hardest part for beginners—focus on feeling your shoulder blades move first, before bending elbows.

Phase 2: Elbow Flexion (3+ inches)

Once the scapula is retracted, elbow flexors (biceps, brachialis) take over. Elbows should stay ~30° from torso for lat engagement.

💪 Primary Muscle Groups

  • Lats (Latissimus Dorsi): 60% of pulling force (why "lean back")
  • Biceps: 25% (secondary, but crucial for full ROM)
  • Rhomboids & Mid-Traps: Scapular retraction and stability
  • Core: Anti-rotation; prevents excessive leg swing

❌ Common Mistakes

  • Dead Hang: Passive scapula = weak pull. Retract FIRST.
  • Flared Elbows: Shifts load to shoulders; impingement risk.
  • Kipping/Swinging: Momentum over control. No ROM gain.
  • Chin Below Bar: Missed range; lower lat engagement.
  • No Chest-Bar Contact: Partial ROM = reduced hypertrophy.

✓ Pro Cues & Corrections

  • "Pull Your Elbows Down" — Better mental cue than "pull yourself up"
  • Dead-Bug Scapular Holds: Practice retracting scapula without moving elbows
  • Pause at Top (2 sec): Ensures full lat activation, not just bicep
  • Negative (Eccentric) Training: Jump to top, lower slowly for 3-5 sec. Builds strength 25% faster.

📈 Progression Path (Zero to One Pull-Up)

  1. Weeks 1-2: Scapular Hangs (30-60 sec); focus on retraction feel
  2. Weeks 3-4: Assisted Pull-Ups (band resistance, 50% body weight)
  3. Weeks 5-6: Eccentric-Only (jump to top, lower 5 sec, repeat 5×)
  4. Weeks 7+: Full Pull-Up attempts; master one before adding volume

Push-Ups: Chest, Shoulder, Core Synergy

Perfect Starting Position

  • Hands: Slightly wider than shoulder width (~1.25x)
  • Arm angle: ~45° from torso (not 90° abduction)
  • Straight line: Heels → Hips → Shoulders → Head (no sag/pike)
  • Core engaged: Glutes tight; posterior pelvic tilt
  • Scapula: Slightly retracted; not shrugged to ears

Lowering (Eccentric)

  • Elbows: Track at ~45° from body (not 90° out or tucked)
  • Chest depth: Chest 2-3 inches from floor
  • Shoulder blades: Slightly retract as you lower (serratus engagement)
  • Body alignment: Stay RIGID; no hips sagging mid-rep

🔬 The Scapulohumeral Rhythm

Push-ups require coordinated scapular & shoulder movement:

Lowering: Scapula retracts & depresses (shoulder blades move down/back)

Pushing: Serratus anterior protracts scapula (spreads shoulder blades) for full ROM

Common fault: Scapular dyskinesis (winging)—shoulder blade lifts away from ribcage, indicates weak serratus.

💪 Muscle Activation (%)

  • Pectoralis Major: 50% (primemover for pressing)
  • Anterior Deltoids: 25% (shoulder flexion assistance)
  • Triceps: 20% (elbow extension)
  • Serratus Anterior: Critical for stability; weak = poor form

❌ Common Form Breakdowns

  • Sagging Hips: Shifts load to shoulders; core disengaged
  • Elbows Out (90°): Shoulder impingement; rotator cuff risk
  • Shallow Depth: Reduced ROM = less pec activation
  • Scapular Winging: Serratus weakness; address with wall-slides
  • Head Forward (CNS): Spinal stress; keep neutral

✓ Advanced Cues for Chest Activation

  • "Spread the Floor" — External rotation cue; activates pec fibers
  • Pause at Bottom (1-2 sec): Increased time under tension; hypertrophy
  • Tempo: 2-1-3 — 2 sec lower, 1 sec pause, 3 sec push (slow & controlled)
  • Archer Push-Ups: One arm does less work → increases difficulty gradually

Planks: Core Anti-Rotation Mastery

🧠 Core Function (Not Just Abs)

Your core's primary job is preventing movement (anti-movement), not creating it:

  • Anti-Extension: Prevents excessive lower back arch (plank load)
  • Anti-Rotation: Prevents torso twisting (obliques + TVA)
  • Anti-Lateral Flexion: Prevents side bending (quadratus lumborum)

Plank: Proper Alignment

Head & Cervical Spine

Neutral (look 12 inches in front of your face). Don't hyperextend neck or tuck chin.

Shoulder Girdle

Elbows directly under shoulders. Scapula slightly retracted (no shrug). Shoulders packed.

Lumbar Spine (Crucial)

Posterior Pelvic Tilt (PPT): Tilt pelvis posteriorly (~10-15°) to flatten lower back. This engages lower abs.

Hip Extension

Glutes should be ACTIVE (slightly clenched). Prevents lumbar hyperextension & hips sagging.

💪 Core Muscles Activated

  • Rectus Abdominis: 40% (prevents lumbar extension)
  • Transverse Abdominis (TVA): 35% (deep stability, intra-abdominal pressure)
  • Internal/External Obliques: 20% (anti-rotation)
  • Erector Spinae (Low Back): 5% (should be QUIET, not hyper-active)

❌ Plank Fails & How to Fix

  • Sagging Hips: Glutes disengaged. Clench glutes hard.
  • Anterior Pelvic Tilt: Lower back arches. Practice PPT first (dead bug).
  • Shoulder Shrug: Trap overuse. Pack shoulders; depress scapula.
  • Head Up (Hyperextension): Compresses cervical spine. Keep neutral gaze.

✓ Plank Progressions & Variations

  1. Basic Plank (Beginner): 20-30 sec holds, 3 sets, focus on form
  2. Plank with Alternating Arm Lifts: Adds anti-rotation challenge
  3. RKC Plank (Advanced): Maximum glute/ab tension; 15-20 sec intense holds
  4. Weighted Plank: Add resistance vest or plate on back (20-50 lbs)
  5. Plank to Downward Dog: Dynamic core + shoulder stability

🔔 Hold Time ≠ Core Strength

A 3-minute sloppy plank with sagging hips is worth less than a 30-second PERFECT plank. Quality > Duration.

Progressive Overload: The Science of Getting Stronger

🎯 The 5 Variables of Progression

1. Volume (Reps × Sets)

Go from 3×5 push-ups → 3×8 → 3×10. +1 rep/week is aggressive but doable.

2. Intensity (Difficulty Level)

Incline push-ups → Regular → Archer → One-arm. Or use resistance bands.

3. Tempo (Speed of Movement)

Slow eccentric (3-5 sec lowering) = 25% more strength gain vs. fast reps.

4. Range of Motion (ROM)

Shallow ROM → Full ROM increases hypertrophy 15-20%. Pause reps at bottom add ROM time.

5. Frequency (Workouts/Week)

3× pull-ups/week > 1× pull-ups/week. But recover properly.

💡 Recommended Weekly Structure

Push Day (Mon): Push-ups (3×8), Dips (3×5), Shoulder press

Pull Day (Wed): Pull-ups (3×5), Rows (3×8), Lat pulldowns

Leg Day (Fri): Squats (4×8), Lunges (3×10), Calf raises

Core (Daily): 5-10 min core work (planks, dead bugs, bird dogs)
