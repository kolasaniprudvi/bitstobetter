What is SIP?

Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is a disciplined way to invest in mutual funds by investing a fixed amount regularly at predetermined intervals. It's designed to help investors build wealth over time through the powerful combination of compounding and rupee cost averaging.

💡 Why SIP Works

SIP leverages two powerful wealth-building principles: compounding (earning returns on your returns) and rupee cost averaging (buying more units when prices are low, fewer when high). This combination reduces market timing risk and maximizes long-term growth.

The SIP Formula Explained

FV = P × [((1 + r)^n - 1) / r] × (1 + r)

Where P = Monthly SIP amount, r = Monthly return rate (annual rate ÷ 12), n = Total number of months

This formula calculates the future value of your SIP investments, accounting for the compound interest earned on each monthly installment. The final (1 + r) factor accounts for the fact that each SIP installment earns returns for a slightly different period.

Understanding Rupee Cost Averaging

Rupee cost averaging is the key benefit of SIP. When you invest a fixed amount regularly:

  • In falling markets: You buy more units at lower NAV (Net Asset Value)
  • In rising markets: You buy fewer units at higher NAV
  • Result: Your average cost per unit tends to be lower than the average NAV over time

📈 Power of Compounding

Starting early is crucial. A ₹5,000 monthly SIP at 12% for 30 years grows to ₹1.76 crore, while the same SIP for 20 years grows to only ₹49 lakh. The extra 10 years adds ₹1.27 crore through compounding!

SIP vs Lumpsum Investment

Aspect SIP Lumpsum
Cash Flow Regular small investments One-time large investment
Market Timing Reduces timing risk Requires timing skill
Discipline Builds saving habit Requires lumpsum availability
Best For Salaried investors Bonus/windfall recipients

Choosing the Right SIP Amount

  • Start with 20% of income: A good rule of thumb for beginners
  • Increase annually: Raise your SIP amount as your income grows (Step-Up SIP)
  • Consider goals: Align SIP amount with your financial goals and timeline
  • Emergency fund first: Build 3-6 months expenses before starting SIP

Common SIP Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stopping SIP during market downturns: This is when rupee cost averaging works best
  • Starting too late: Every year of delay costs significantly in compounding
  • Investing without goals: Link SIPs to specific financial objectives
  • Ignoring expense ratios: Lower expense ratios mean higher net returns
  • Over-diversifying: Too many funds dilute focus and increase tracking difficulty

SIP Frequency Options

While monthly SIP is most common, you can choose:

  • Daily SIP: Maximum rupee cost averaging, but administrative complexity
  • Weekly SIP: Good frequency with manageable complexity
  • Monthly SIP: Most popular, aligns with salary cycles
  • Quarterly SIP: Suitable for investors with irregular income
